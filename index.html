In Mail Art and the Big Monster, Ulises Carrión explores the meaning behind creating "mail art" and the impact that creating such art has on society. As Carrión states, "Mail Art uses the mail as support in the sense that non­ mail arts use canvas, paper. iron, and wood as support". Carrión continues to reaffirm and expound upon this statement, reaffirming the idea that artists must not use the mail system as a medium for art, but rather as a support they can build upon to create art using a multitude of media. While doing so, Carrión seems to believe that artists who limit themselves to creating postcards are not truly "Mail artists". I'm unsure if I agree with this idea, since art itself is often subjective and up to the audience/artist's interpretation.

Carrión describes the "Big Monster" as "the owner of the Castle, who separates us one from the others, all of us". Although Carrión does not give a clear definition as to what the "Big Monster" represents in this passage, Carrión's writing allows us to infer that the "Big Monster" may be the "system" that artists and non-artists alike struggle to combat. In the case of Mail Art, this may relate more to the government and the policies it enacts that may not benefit citizens as a whole. However, I think Mail Art may also feed the system it fights against, as artists must abide by the rules and regulations the "Big Monster" creates when creating "mail art".

Discussion Questions:

How does an artist's interpretation of "Mail Art" affect the quality of the art they produce?

While Carrión writes that through Mail Art artists are part of the "guerrilla war against the Big Monster", does Mail Art also feed and support the Big Monster along the way by abiding by the system it creates?

In Alt-Text as Poetry Workbook, Coklyat and Finnegan describe the many usages alt-text can serve and the many different approaches one can take to writing alt-text. In particular, the passage discusses the purpose of alt-text in regards to the needs of the intended audience. This is in contrast to the "alt-text as poetry" mindset that the passage also covers and encourages the reader to consider. I agree with the authors that thinking of alt-text as merely an alternative description of an image severely limits the creative decisions one can take with writing alt-text, especially because an image may not only be made up of purely visual descriptions, but may also evoke specific emotions within the audience. By limiting the alt-text to only describing the visual elements of an image, the writer loses the context of the image within the source and prevents the alt-text readers from engaging in the entire experience. While images are made up of colors and linework that present an idea to the audience, poetry is made up of words and phrases that are meant to similarly portray an idea or evoke a specific medium. Targeting each artistic medium to a specific environment enables the creator better express their thoughts to a wider audience in general.

In Art on the Internet, Boris Groys raises the question of whether too much context, in regards to knowledge about an art piece or artist, obstructs the traditional critique and appreciation of art. Groys describes this process as "defictionalization", where the frames that devices such as phones and computers create are inescapable and thus prevent users from fully experiencing art on the internet. I thought that the "globalization of the author", as Groys describes, is an interesting point of view on how art is treated much like a business nowadays rather than a process of creation. Since your audience can be anyone and everyone, many artists focus on "putting themselves out there" to better share their content with others. However, Groys argues that in order to survive in this culture nowadays, an artist must not only be globally present but also locally familiar. I also thought the section about "surveillance" to be particularly relatable. Many artists that I follow online have posted work-in-progress drawings, speedpaints, and timelapses that illuminate their creative process. While I have always seen this as a positive aspect of the internet, as I often draw inspiration from their creative processes and am interested in applying their methods to creating my own art, the passage points towards the "objectification" of artists on the internet. As Groys states, "creative work is creative because it takes place beyond public control - and even beyond the conscious control of the author". The creative process is an integral part of an artist's identity, and the internet, in making it public, takes away that part of the identity.

Questions:

In the world of today, commodification of art and the artistic process is unavoidable. What steps can be taken or have already been taken to try and hinder this process?

Groys views the power of the Internet in decontexualizing and recontexualizing historical art via "cut-and-paste" operations in a positive light. Do you agree?
